Introduction

Kristina Stemikovskaya is a researcher associated with TECNALIA Research & Innovation, one of the leading applied research centres in Europe. Her work sits at the intersection of artificial intelligence and healthcare, with a particular focus on the challenges that arise when machine learning methods are deployed in real-world clinical and biomedical contexts.

Her research addresses a field that is both technically demanding and socially significant: designing AI systems that are capable of learning from complex, noisy, and scarce biomedical signals while simultaneously respecting the privacy constraints inherent to medical data. This dual commitment — to technical rigor and to clinical applicability — defines the character of her scientific work.

Her background is notably interdisciplinary. Earlier in her career, her work touched on statistical methodology, including the study of empirical copula processes, which reflects a grounding in rigorous mathematical foundations. This trajectory has evolved towards applied AI for health, where the same concern for formal correctness is brought to bear on problems of genuine clinical urgency.

Research Overview

A broad yet coherent programme spanning biomedical signal processing, self-supervised learning, and privacy-preserving AI

Stemikovskaya's research programme can be understood as a sustained effort to make machine learning genuinely useful in healthcare settings — not merely as an academic exercise, but as a set of methods capable of operating under the real constraints that clinical environments impose. Those constraints are severe: medical data is scarce, highly heterogeneous, often confidential, and collected under conditions that are difficult to standardise.

Her work engages with biomedical data in multiple forms, with a particular emphasis on time series arising from physiological processes. Among the signals she studies, electroencephalography (EEG) occupies a central role. EEG recordings capture electrical activity in the brain at high temporal resolution, making them invaluable for detecting neurological events — but also notoriously difficult to analyse automatically due to their complexity, noise, and variability across individuals.

Seizure detection is a core clinical application within her research. Identifying epileptic seizures reliably and automatically in continuous EEG streams remains an open problem, particularly when models are required to generalise across patients. Her work also extends to brain-machine interfaces (BMI), where decoding algorithms are used to interpret neural signals for rehabilitation and assistance applications.

A transversal theme throughout her research is the problem of data scarcity and privacy. In response, she investigates synthetic data generation as a means of augmenting training sets, and privacy-preserving federated learning as a framework for enabling collaborative model training across hospitals and institutions without sharing raw patient data.

Current Research

Contrastive learning methods for EEG, cross-patient generalisation, and trustworthy AI in federated medical settings

Current public information indicates that Stemikovskaya's most recent work focuses intensely on self-supervised learning for EEG analysis, particularly in the context of automatic seizure detection. Self-supervised approaches are especially attractive in biomedical settings because they can extract rich representations from large volumes of unlabelled EEG recordings, circumventing the bottleneck of expert annotation that limits conventional supervised methods.

A key methodological contribution of her recent work is the development of frequency-aware contrastive learning for EEG. Standard contrastive learning frameworks treat all signal augmentations symmetrically, but EEG signals carry clinically meaningful information at specific frequency bands — delta, theta, alpha, beta, and gamma rhythms each correspond to distinct neurological states. Her work appears to incorporate this domain knowledge directly into the contrastive objective, allowing the model to learn representations that are sensitive to frequency-level structure in neural signals.

A persistent challenge in EEG-based AI is cross-patient generalisation: models trained on one patient or cohort frequently fail when applied to new individuals, whose brain signals may differ substantially in morphology, amplitude, and spectral characteristics. Her research addresses this challenge by designing learning frameworks that promote more robust and transferable representations.

In parallel, her work on trustworthy federated learning in medical settings reflects a recognition that the deployment of AI in healthcare cannot be reduced to predictive performance alone. Federated systems operating across clinical institutions must contend with heterogeneous data distributions, fairness across subpopulations, Byzantine robustness, and transparent accountability — all of which are active concerns in her research agenda.

Why This Research Area Matters

The scientific and practical case for AI in healthcare, EEG analysis, and privacy-sensitive machine learning

The application of artificial intelligence to healthcare represents one of the most consequential frontiers in current research. The potential benefits are substantial: earlier and more accurate diagnosis, reduced burden on clinical staff, more personalised treatment, and AI-assisted monitoring of patients in settings where continuous human supervision is impractical. Yet realising these benefits requires confronting a set of fundamental challenges that distinguish healthcare AI from its counterparts in other domains.

01

Scarcity of Labelled Medical Data

Unlike image recognition or natural language processing, most biomedical domains lack large annotated datasets. Labelling clinical data — whether EEG recordings, histological images, or patient records — requires scarce expert time and is prone to inter-annotator variability. This scarcity makes standard supervised learning brittle, and motivates exploration of self-supervised, semi-supervised, and synthetic data methods that can extract value from unlabelled or minimally labelled corpora.

02

Patient Variability and Generalisation

Physiological signals are profoundly individual. EEG morphology, spectral content, and noise characteristics vary enormously between patients, across recording sessions, and even within a single session. A model trained on data from one cohort may fail entirely when applied to a new population. Building AI systems that generalise robustly across patients and clinical sites is a central open problem in biomedical AI.

03

Privacy Restrictions in Medical Machine Learning

Medical data is among the most sensitive information generated about individuals. Legal frameworks such as the GDPR impose strict constraints on its collection, storage, and transfer. These constraints make it extremely difficult to accumulate the large, multi-site datasets that powerful AI models typically require. Federated learning and privacy-preserving techniques offer a principled path forward, but they introduce their own challenges related to communication efficiency, model convergence, and resistance to inference attacks.

04

Reliability and Clinical Trust

For an AI system to be usable in a clinical setting, predictive accuracy alone is insufficient. Clinicians require tools that are interpretable, robust to distribution shift, calibrated in their confidence estimates, and auditable. The emerging field of trustworthy AI seeks to formalise these requirements and develop evaluation frameworks aligned with regulatory standards, including the EU AI Act and domain-specific medical device regulations.

05

EEG Analysis and Seizure Detection at Scale

Epilepsy affects over 50 million people worldwide. Continuous EEG monitoring generates hours of data per patient, far exceeding the capacity of manual expert review. Automated seizure detection could transform clinical monitoring by enabling real-time alerts, reducing review time, and supporting remote or ambulatory monitoring. Yet the complexity and variability of ictal EEG patterns make this a genuinely hard computational problem, particularly in the absence of labels and when models must transfer to unseen patients.

Frequency-Aware Contrastive Loss for Self-Supervised EEG Seizure Detection

This work addresses one of the core limitations in EEG-based seizure detection: the absence of large labelled datasets for supervised training. By formulating a contrastive learning objective that is explicitly aware of the frequency structure of EEG signals, the paper enables models to learn clinically meaningful representations from unlabelled recordings. The key insight is that different frequency bands in EEG carry distinct neurological information, and a learning signal that respects this structure produces more transferable and clinically useful features. The work reports improved performance on seizure detection benchmarks, particularly in cross-patient evaluation settings. It represents a significant methodological step in applying self-supervised learning to biomedical time series with strong domain structure.

Applications and Impact

From clinical monitoring to collaborative hospital AI — the practical reach of research at this intersection

⚡ Epilepsy Monitoring

Automated and continuous EEG-based seizure detection can reduce the burden of manual clinical review, enable real-time alerting for patients at risk, and support ambulatory monitoring outside hospital settings. Self-supervised models capable of generalising across patients could make such systems clinically viable even in the absence of patient-specific annotated training data.

🏥 Clinical Decision Support

AI-assisted interpretation of physiological signals can support neurologists and intensivists in time-critical decision-making, particularly in intensive care settings where EEG monitoring is used continuously. Reliable, explainable AI tools can serve as second-opinion systems, flagging events for clinical review and reducing the risk of missed diagnoses during long monitoring sessions.

🦾 Neurorehabilitation

Brain-machine interface technologies enabled by robust neural decoding algorithms have direct applications in rehabilitation for patients with motor impairments following stroke, spinal cord injury, or neurodegenerative disease. More generalised and adaptive BMI systems — requiring less patient-specific calibration — could make these technologies accessible to a broader clinical population.

🔐 Privacy-Preserving Medical AI

Federated learning frameworks allow AI models to be trained on distributed patient data across multiple hospitals without centralising sensitive records. This architecture is increasingly relevant as healthcare systems seek to harness large-scale data while complying with data protection legislation and maintaining patient trust. Research on trustworthy federated AI directly addresses these systemic needs.

🤝 Collaborative Hospital-Based Learning

Multi-site federated training enables hospitals to collectively build more powerful and generalisable AI systems than any single institution could produce independently. This is especially valuable for rare conditions or minority patient populations where data at any single site is insufficient for model development. Synthetic data generation can complement federated approaches by augmenting local datasets and enabling safe data sharing.

Summary

Kristina Stemikovskaya represents a scientific profile that is increasingly valued in the current landscape of applied AI research: one that combines formal methodological rigour with a clear orientation towards real-world clinical problems. Her trajectory — from statistical theory to self-supervised EEG analysis and federated medical AI — reflects a coherent intellectual arc driven by the question of how machine learning can be made both technically sound and genuinely deployable in healthcare.

Her work on frequency-aware contrastive learning for EEG places her at the frontier of a rapidly evolving area: the application of self-supervised representation learning to biomedical signals. This field is attracting substantial attention precisely because it offers a principled response to the label scarcity problem that has historically limited AI in clinical neurology. By incorporating domain knowledge — specifically the spectral organisation of neural signals — into the learning objective, her approach moves beyond generic contrastive frameworks and towards methods that are genuinely adapted to the structure of the data they process.

More broadly, her simultaneous engagement with trustworthy federated learning, synthetic data, and cross-patient generalisation situates her work at the confluence of several major trends in contemporary AI research: the turn towards privacy-preserving distributed learning, the growing emphasis on reliability and accountability in AI systems, and the expanding toolkit of self-supervised methods. Taken together, these commitments define a research programme that is technically ambitious, clinically motivated, and well-aligned with the direction in which healthcare AI is moving.
